China before Reforms

Under the leadership of Mao Zedong, China operated a planned economy in which a large part of the economy was controlled by the state. Prices were determined, production targets set, and resources allocated. The economy focused on self-sufficiency; products were only imported from other countries if they could not be produced or obtained domestically.

Key initiatives included the collectivization of farms into communes and state-sponsored industrialization. However, all these policies led to a lack of incentive for companies, workers, and farmers to care about the quality of their products. Instead, they were solely focused on meeting production targets.

China suffered from significant economic difficulties, especially during the "Great Leap Forward" (a failed campaign by Mao Zedong to catch up with Western countries) from 1958 to 1962, which also triggered a famine that resulted in the deaths of up to 45 million people. The standard of living dropped by 20.3% during this period. Compared to countries like Japan, the growth of the standard of living was very low.

In 1978, after the death of Mao Zedong in 1976, China began to liberalize its market and open up trade with the West, hoping this would promote economic growth and improve living standards. Deng Xiaoping, who held the role of "paramount leader," initiated these reforms.
